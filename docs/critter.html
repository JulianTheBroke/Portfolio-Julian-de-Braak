C.R.I.T.T.E.R.

Lead Programmer · GameMaker · Team of 5

Discover language from the critters that roam the forest as you run, jump, and swing through it.

They speak in words you do not understand at first. As you collect language stones, meaning starts to click, and you can complete tasks for the critters you meet along the way.

My Contribution

What I Did

Programming & World

  • Lead programmer on a shared codebase, coded most gameplay and systems
  • Built the forest levels from team sprites; teammates handled pixel art and dialogue writing
  • Composed music, kept builds runnable, and ran playtests through iteration

LDtk Pipeline

  • Designed level layout in LDtk, then wrote a converter tool to generate GameMaker rooms from the export
  • Mapped entities (player, checkpoints, collectibles, NPCs) into the right objects in-engine

Language & Dialogue

  • Critters speak in ALL-CAPS vocabulary words; unknown words stay highlighted and learned words show their meaning
  • Language stones add words to global.learned_words, and the dialogue translator re-renders lines as you progress
  • Quest dialogue and NPC tasks gate on what you understand, so you need certain words before a critter's request makes sense

Mechanics

  • State machine for player modes: free, dash, wall slide, grapple, reading
  • Dash, glide, and grapple unlock through forest quests; movement uses coyote time, jump buffer, and variable jump height
Core Loop

How It Plays

  1. Explore the forest: run, jump, wall slide, and swing on your tail
  2. Find language stones and learn critter words one by one
  3. Return to NPCs as the dialogue shifts from unreadable to translated
  4. Complete tasks for forest critters; new abilities let you reach areas you could not before
  5. Push deeper into the forest, toward the demo's end

Reflection

What I'd Improve

Clearer accountability on a five-person team. Roles blurred early and I picked up work outside programming to keep the demo moving.
More playtesting on language and ability pacing. The gap between understanding enough to follow a quest and actually reaching the new areas needed tighter tuning.
A writer-friendly pipeline for critter vocabulary. The word dictionary lived in code; a shared table would have made it easier for teammates to add and review dialogue.
